Abstract:
Ecosystem-based, marine spatial planning offers great opportunity to place information representing multiple management objectives into a flexible decision support framework. This dataset is part of a larger framework used to represent biodiversity conservation and coastal hazard objectives in Suffolk County, New York, USA. It is called Coastal Resilience.
The Long Island shores have highly developed lands and valuable marine resources in the coastal zone. Much of the private property is only inches above sea level, placing millions of dollars in public and private funds at risk. This also threatens wetlands and other coastal ecosystems that provide habitat, natural buffers to storms and other services. Despite a growing awareness of global climate change, local decision makers still lack the tools to examine different management objectives as sea levels rise and coastal hazards increase. The Coastal Resilience project was designed to address these issues by providing tools to better inform decision-making.
Coastal resilience describes the self-organizing ability of a coast to respond in a sustainable manner to morphological, biological and/or socio-economic pressures. A primary goal of the project is to design, build and discuss future climatic scenarios that account for sea level rise and storm surge while also identifying human communities that are vulnerable and at risk. The project also addresses the role of natural communities such as tidal marshes that may be more resilient to climatic stress and thereby provide protection to humans if they in turn are afforded protection. The Nature Conservancy, working with partners including the NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies, the Association of State Floodplain Managers, the Pace Land Use Law Center and NOAA's Coastal Services Center have delivered this information via an internet mapping application to help local decision makers keep the environment and public safety in mind.
Coastal Resilience Long Island (<http://coastalresilience.org>) is a unique interactive web mapping tool that provides three types of information to meet this need. First, it allows users to visualize the issue by exploring flooding scenarios resulting from sea level rise and storm surge. Second, users can see what?s at risk by viewing the interaction of flooding with ecological and socioeconomic resources. Finally, Coastal Resilience helps users identify what can be done to meet the joint objectives of ecosystem protection and community resilience. The information in Coastal Resilience is presented without hype or hyperbole in a user-friendly framework designed to inform the decision making process.

Process_Description:
Social Vulnerability Index (SVI)- Socioeconomic data were obtained from the 2000 US Census SF3 Data and a suite of indicators were mapped at the census block group scale. For each indicator, the block groups were ranked from most vulnerable to least vulnerable. The ranks for each indicator were summed for each block group and standardized. The resulting SVI illustrates the relative social vulnerability of each block group with the lowest values being most indicative of the most socially vulnerable block groups. Critical Facilities and Infrastructure Index (CFII)- Facilities and infrastructure data were obtained from the US Census, New York State GIS, and the National Institute of Building Sciences. Counts and/or length were tallied for each blockgroup to determine how much infrastructure and facilities were within each block group. For each indicator, the block groups were ranked from the greatest number of facilities or infrastructure to least amount of facilities or infrastructure. The ranks for each type of facility or infrastructure were summed for each block group and standardized. The resulting CFII illustrates the relative critical facilities and infrastructure vulnerability of each block group with the lowest values being indicative of the block groups containing the most infrastructure and facilities. Overall Community Vulnerability Index (CVI)- Combined Social Vulnerability and Critical Facilities and Infrastructure Indices. The overall community vulnerability index represents the combined vulnerability of the people, property, and resources within a community. To create the CVI, we added the two indices and standardized the resulting index.

Attribute_Definition:
The social vulnerability index is based on a suite of socioeconomic indicators identified in peer-reviewed literature that appear to make communities more or less susceptible to disaster impacts. The social vulnerability index ranks block groups to help identify special consideration areas where access to resources for coping with hazards may be extremely limited. Socioeconomic data were obtained from the 2000 US Census SF3 Data.
Attribute:
Attribute_Label: INF_STND_R
Attribute_Definition:
The critical infrastructure and facilities index ranks census block groups based on the amount of critical infrastructure and facilities located within each block group. The critical infrastructure and facilities index allows identification of block groups with large amounts of infrastructure and facilities that may be at risk. Infrastructure and facilities data were obtained from the US Census and the National Institute of Building Sciences.
